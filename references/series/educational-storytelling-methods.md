# Educational Storytelling Methods

This note applies to the chip, robot, drone, AR, and future technology series.
The goal is to make viewers learn because they want to know what happens next.

## Principle

Do not start with an explanation. Start with a problem that can only be solved by
understanding one concept.

Good:

```text
The wafer moon has 100 tiny chip squares. Some turn gray after Sasa sneezes.
Aya asks why only some survive. The word card reveals "yield".
```

Weak:

```text
Aya explains semiconductor yield while everyone stands still.
```

## The Five-Beat Tech Story

1. **Wonder**: show a beautiful physical version of the concept.
2. **Mistake**: Lala Xia or Sasa Kun misunderstands the word.
3. **Constraint**: the world refuses to move until the concept is understood.
4. **Tiny Explanation**: Aya or LazyingArtRobot gives one clear sentence.
5. **Payoff**: the explanation changes the action and unlocks the next scene.

## Keep Concepts Physical

Turn abstract technology into props:

- `yield`: green and gray chip squares after testing.
- `cache`: a tiny snack drawer close to the chef.
- `bandwidth`: a crowded bridge carrying data boxes.
- `latency`: an AR arrow that arrives late.
- `haptics`: a robot finger learning not to crush a lychee.
- `SLAM`: a drone drawing the room while finding itself.
- `ISA`: a recipe book that tells hardware what commands mean.

## Make The Joke Teach

The joke should reveal the concept:

- Lala Xia thinks `yield` means politely giving away snacks.
- Sasa Kun thinks `mask` means a face mask and wears the lithography mask.
- LazyingArtRobot treats "give me a hand" literally.
- Aya Chan corrects the meaning by showing the physical consequence.

If the joke could be removed without losing the lesson, rewrite it.

## Use The Words Card

The words card should appear once per episode as a real prop:

- on the cleanroom bench;
- beside a drone landing pad;
- held by LazyingArtRobot;
- pinned as an AR object through LightMind glasses;
- used as a tiny key or ticket.

It should show one word only. Do not use it to explain the whole episode.

## Tension Without Darkness

For LALACHAN, technical failure should be exciting but not grim:

- a wafer may flicker, not explode violently;
- a drone may stop before soup, not injure anyone;
- a robot hand may squeeze a strawberry, not harm a character;
- an AR arrow may cause a cushion crash, not danger.

The viewer should feel: "I want to learn the concept so the cute scene works."

## Continuation Hooks

End each episode with a small technical doorway:

- yield problem opens cleanroom contamination episode;
- mask pattern error opens transistor-city traffic episode;
- cache drawer opens memory-bandwidth episode;
- drone map reveals hidden lab door;
- robot hand unlocks a tactile key;
- AR card reveals an anchored route.

The hook should be visual, not verbal.
